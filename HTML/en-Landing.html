Mercalli earthquake scale

The Mercalli scale, also known as the Mercalli-Mili scale, represents one of the older ways of measuring the strength of earthquakes. Developed during the 19th century, this scale was originally based on observations and subjective experiences of people after the earthquake. It was later modified and expanded by an American engineer and seismologist Charles F. Milley.

The scale consists of ten degrees, numbered from 1 to 10, pri where each degree describes different levels of earthquake intensity. This scale is specifically focused on describing the impact of earthquakes on human environment and objects, instead of measuring physical characteristics ground shaking itself.

At entry levels, such as "Micro" (1) or "Minor" (2), earthquakes are usually imperceptible or felt by few people without causing damage. With increasing degrees, like "Moderate" (5) or "Strong" (6), the intensity of the earthquake is increasing, with the possibility of serious ones damage to buildings and the environment. The highest level, "Extreme" (10), means complete destruction, severe damage or collapse of all building in the affected area.

It is important to note that, although the Mercalli scale was significant in past, modern approaches to seismic evaluation often use others methods like Earthquake Magnitude and Earthquake Intensity. Magnitude measures the energy released during an earthquake, while Intensity estimates the effects of an earthquake at a specific location.

In conclusion, the Mercalli Scale remains part of the history of seismology and contributed to the understanding of the intensity of earthquakes, although today often combined with more modern methods for more precise analysis it shakes.
